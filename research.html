Current Research

The past 20 years have seen a dramatic expansion in our knowledge of stars <100 million years old which lie within 100 pc of the Sun, i.e., closer than the nearest well-studied star-forming regions.

My research is centred around identifying and better understanding our young stellar neighbours, particularly at the lowest masses and youngest ages. These systems provide unique tests of pre-main sequence stellar evolution and the late stages of protoplanetary disc evolution, and represent some of the best targets for direct imaging of giant exoplanets.

Below are just two of the interesting topics my collaborators and I are currently working on. If you are interested in joining us at UNSW Canberra to continue this work, please get it touch to find out more!

'Peter Pan' discs around juvenile stars


Artist's impression of the 'Peter Pan' circumstellar disc around WISE J080822.18-644357.3 (Credit: NASA Goddard Space Flight Center/Jonathan Holden).

A 'Peter Pan' disc is a new type of circumstellar disc which retains its gas and dust for tens of millions of years, much longer than the normal dissipation timescale of a few million years. In other words, a disc that refused to 'grow up'!

Starting with the discovery and our confirmation and detailed characterisation of the Peter Pan disc around WISE J080822.18-644357.3, there are now at least half a dozen juvenile (>20 million year old), low-mass stars with near-infrared excesses and accretion signatures indicating the presence of warm dust and gas. Among them is our recent discovery of the oldest (55 million year-old) low-mass star known to host ongoing accretion, 2MASS J15460752-6258042. It exhibits strong and variable optical emission lines (e.g. Hα, He I, [OI]) as well as an infrared excess in the 11 and 24 μm WISE bands.

The simplest model to explain the existence of Peter Pan discs is that primordial (i.e. first generation) discs around M-dwarfs take longer to dissipate than their higher-mass cousins, a trend also observed in younger star-forming regions. This would mean that the time available for planet formation around M-dwarfs is longer than previously thought, permitting the formation of gas giant planets at relatively late ages, in contradiction to most current planet formation theories.



The spectral energy distribution of the disc around WISE J0808 from Flaherty et al. (2019). The blue and red blackbody components with temperatures of 1071 K and 237 K are necessary to explain the mid-infrared excess (Murphy et al. 2018), while a colder 20 K component is needed to reproduce the long-wavelength ALMA emission. This system is the prototypical example of star hosting a Peter Pan disc.

To better understand the gas and dust properties of WISE J0808 we obtained ALMA observations of the system which did not show the presence of cold gas, seemingly ruling out a gas-rich protoplanetary disc. From the continuum emission we could infer the presence of about 0.06 MEarth of optically-thin dust, consistent with being generated through a collisional cascade of large planetesimals. This scenario still requires a long-lived gas component to explain the observed accretion signatures, which raises the question of why gas has persisted around these particular stars and not others?

Obtaining robust age estimates is crucial in the characterisation of a Peter Pan system. This involves a thorough, holistic analysis of the available photometric, spectroscopic and astrometric (e.g. Gaia) data for each star. Kinematically tying a star to a moving group or association of known age is particularly useful, as we were able to do with WISE J0808 and the Carina Association. With Gaia's high precision astrometry available for millions of nearby stars, determining the true prevalence and evolutioanry status of Peter Pan discs will soon be possible.


The young groups η and ε Chamaeleontis


Infrared Astronomical Satellite (IRAS) 25/60/100 micron colour composite of the Chamaeleon region, showing the young open cluster η Cha, the ε Cha Association and the background Chamaeleon dark clouds.

Two of the most intriguing and understudied young moving groups in the Southern sky are the open cluster η Cha and the ε Cha Association. Both are <10 million years old and only 100 pc away, making them some of the youngest stars in the Solar neighbourhood.

For the past several years we have been conducting a systematic investigation of these groups and the young stars in their vicinity. This has already lead to several notable discoveries:

  • The low-mass stellar halo around η Cha
    The central core of η Cha appears to be missing many of its low-mass members, ostensibly due to dynamical evolution which dispersed the stars to large radii. From a wide-field (95 sq. deg) photometric and proper motion survey around η Cha, combined with extensive follow-up spectroscopy, we have identified several new young, low-mass stars up to 5 degrees from η Cha. These stars have proper motions and radial velocities consistent with ejection or evaporation from the cluster over the past 5-10 Myr.
  • Episodic accretion in 'old' pre-main sequence clusters
    Several members of η and ε Cha show strong and variable H-alpha emission in our multi-epoch ANU 2.3-m/WiFeS spectra, including one star which underwent an isolated event indicative of accretion from a circumstellar disc. WISE infrared photometry confirms the presence of a disc around the star and demonstrates that episodic accretion can continue at low levels long after the majority of circumstellar material has dissipated, much like in Peter Pan systems.
  • A new wide pre-main sequence binary born in Sco-Cen
    Two of the outlying η Cha candidates are noticeably older than the cluster and only 42 arcsec apart. We have shown that they almost certainly form an isolated, wide (4000–5000 AU) 10 Myr-old binary. The dynamical fragility of the system puts strong constraints on its birthplace, which we believe was in the outskirts of the Sco-Cen OB association.

Young eclipsing binary


Credit: J. Pinfield/Uni. of Hertfordshire.

My co-authors and I recently published the first detailed examination of the 24 million year-old M-dwarf eclipsing binary THOR 42.

To better measure the masses, radii, temperatures and ages of the two stars we obtained observations from the ANU 2.3-m telescope, the Magellan 6.5-m in Chile and the TESS satellite, among other facilities. THOR 42 is now one of the most precisely characterized pre-main sequence eclipsing binaries known, and a vital touchstone object against which to test theoretical models of stellar evolution.

 THOR 42: A touchstone 24 Myr-old eclipsing binary spanning the fully convective boundary
Murphy, S. J. et al. 2020, MNRAS, 491(4), 4902.


The disappearing disc


Credit: Gemini Observatory/AURA, Lynette Cook

In 2012 we reported in Nature the curious case of a young, Sun-like star (TYC 8241-2652-1) whose infrared flux decreased by a factor of 30 in less than two years. This is orders of magnitude faster than models of circumstellar disc dissipation predict.

The approximately 10 million year-old star seems to have gone from hosting substantial quantities of warm, dusty material to retaining only a meagre amount of cooler dust. Such a rapid transformation has not been observed in any other star, and so far no physical model can satisfactorily explain all the observations.

Rapid disappearance of a warm, dusty circumstellar disk
Melis, C., Zuckerman, B., Rhee, J. H., Song, I., Murphy, S. J. & Bessell, M. S., 2012, Nature, 487, 74


Meeting the neighbours


Credit: Adric Riedel, GSU/RECONS/SMARTS

Adric Riedel (STScI), Carl Melis (UCSD) and I discovered that the M4.5 flare star AP Columbae is in fact the closest (8.4 pc) young star to the Sun, with an estimated age of only 40 million years. It is a member of a young group of stars called the Argus Association.

This combination of youth and proximity makes AP Col an ideal target for direct detection of extra-solar planets. However, high-contrast imaging on the Very Large Telescope in Chile and the Keck telescopes in Hawaii has ruled out any Jupiter-mass companions at projected separations of greater than 3–4 AU.

The Solar Neighborhood. XXVI. AP Col: The Closest (8.4 pc) Pre-main-sequence Star
Riedel, A. R., Murphy, S. J., Henry, T. J., Melis, C., Jao, W.-C. & Subasavage, J. P., 2011, AJ, 142, 104
